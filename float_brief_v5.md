## 1. The Problem

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health problem in children and adolescents — affecting as many as one in three teens. CBT is the gold standard treatment, and its central component is structured, graduated exposure work. But the specific exposure-based model that actually produces results is practiced by only a small number of specialists nationwide.

Most practitioners — school social workers, school psychologists, community therapists — either lack proper training in exposure-based CBT or are using approaches that feel therapeutic but have significantly weaker outcomes. Many practitioners who believe they are delivering CBT are not using the exposure-based model that works. Others know the gap exists and refer to specialists, but availability is severely limited.

The result is a large and growing population of anxious children and teens who are not getting the treatment that would help them. This is not primarily a supply problem — it is a knowledge and method problem.

Parents are a critical and underutilized part of the treatment picture. Research shows that family accommodation — parental and sibling behaviors that reduce a child's distress — actively maintains and worsens anxiety over time. This is nearly universal: 95–100% of parents of anxious youth engage in accommodation behaviors regularly, often daily. Most parents don't know this, and most treatment models don't address it systematically.

---

## 2. The Solution

Float is a practitioner-led clinical platform that puts Dr. Walker's proven CBT model in the hands of every school social worker and community therapist treating anxious children and teens.

The platform does not replace the practitioner. It equips them — giving them the structure, tools, and between-session infrastructure to deliver outcomes that currently require specialist expertise. Dr. Walker's clinical model is encoded into the platform's logic, guiding practitioners through the full treatment journey from initial referral through active exposure work.

Float connects three participants in the treatment: the practitioner, the parent, and the child or teen. The practitioner configures and monitors treatment. The parent is an active therapeutic agent with their own tools and behavioral targets. The child does the between-session therapeutic work using the platform. Float is the connective tissue between all three.

## 3. The Clinical Foundation

Float is built on Dr. Bridget Walker's CBT program for anxiety and related disorders in children, adolescents, and young adults, developed and refined through over 25 years of clinical practice. The model is documented in three published books:

- **Anxiety Relief for Kids, 2nd Edition** (New Harbinger, 2026)
- **Social Anxiety Relief for Teens** (New Harbinger, 2021)
- **Anxiety Relief for Kids** (New Harbinger, 2017)

The model centers on a structured process applied across anxiety disorder presentations including social anxiety and OCD/ERP. The core exposure sequence for each trigger situation is:

1. Build a trigger situations list with Distress Thermometer (DT) ratings for each situation
2. Select the situation with the lowest DT rating that occurs frequently
3. Identify the avoidance and safety behaviors the child relies on in that situation
4. Assess the child's DT for being in the trigger situation while refraining from each behavior — each of these ratings becomes a rung on the exposure ladder
5. Use the Downward Arrow technique on each rung to identify the child's feared outcome
6. Arrange the rungs from lowest to highest DT — the resulting Exposure Ladder guides the exposure sequence
7. Begin exposures at the bottom rung, climbing to mastery, then moving up

**Belief in Prediction (BIP)** is derived from the Downward Arrow: after identifying the child's most feared outcome, ask how strongly they believe that outcome will occur (0–100%).

**Confidence Level** is assessed before each exposure: how confident is the child they can do it — low, medium, or high. This is distinct from BIP (what they believe will happen) and DT (how distressing they expect it to be).

**Two primary outcome measures** are tracked: Distress Thermometer (0–10) and BIP (0–100%). The terminology is Distress Thermometer — not SUDS — because some anxious children present with discomfort or unease rather than overt fear.

## 4. Dr. Walker's Clinical Workflow

This section documents the actual session-by-session workflow as practiced by Dr. Walker. Platform design follows this sequence exactly.

### Pre-consultation: Referral and monitoring

1. Person is referred to Dr. Walker — email address acquired
2. **Monitoring form sent to parent** — parent monitors child's anxiety triggers, behaviors, and distress levels for one week and submits results through Float
3. Float generates a **pre-consultation report** from the monitoring data for Dr. Walker to reference in the first meeting

### Consultation 1: Parents only (approx. 1 hour)

No child or teenager present. Ideally both parents attend.

- Assess type of anxiety presentation
- Educate parents about CBT model — including the parental accommodation role and the Nickname concept
- Review monitoring form data
- Capture in-session notes (practitioner-only, free text)
- No platform actions taken by the family at this stage

Following this meeting, Dr. Walker sends educational material to the parents as a follow-up.

### Decision point

Parent reconnects with Dr. Walker and confirms they want to move forward with treatment.

### Consultation 2: Kid/teen + parents (age-dependent)

- Review CBT education with the child/teen — use examples from the monitoring form and prior session notes
- Introduce the Distress Thermometer and other key concepts
- Discuss rewards with parents
- Create a Nickname for the anxiety
- **One action item only**: the child starts using the Nickname between sessions

### Consultation 3: First clinical work

- Review trigger situations — informed by monitoring data and parent input
- Establish DT ratings for each trigger situation
- Select the lowest-DT situation that occurs frequently
- Identify avoidance and safety behaviors for that situation
- Assess DT for refraining from each behavior
- Pick one behavior with a lower/lowest DT
- Plan the first exposure:
  - Confidence level (low / medium / high)
  - BIP rating
  - Scheduled time(s) — specific, with a few options per day
  - Patient commits to the exposure (explicit commit action)
- Generate action plan for the child/teen
- Generate action plan for the parent

### Active treatment: Weekly sessions

From this point, sessions are typically weekly. Between sessions:

- Patient runs exposures, records results
- Practitioner receives completion notifications or missed experiment alerts
- Parent receives notifications if consent given
- Practitioner messages patient as needed
- Ladder builds out over time — may cover more than one trigger situation

### Exposure workflow requirements

Each exposure must:
1. **Be defined** — with the clinician
2. **Be scheduled** — specific times, reviewed by practitioner
3. **Have a commit action** — patient explicitly commits before running it
4. **Have an adjust option** — patient can flag "too hard" and trigger a conversation to make it more manageable
5. **Be recorded** — results captured with DT, BIP, confidence, feared outcome assessment
6. **Have accountability** — did you do it or not, with follow-up if not

## 5. The Clinical Toolkit

All tools are accessible to practitioners, parents, and children/teens. None are practitioner-only. Practice exercises available for each user type.

### Psychoeducation Tools
- **Distress Thermometer (DT)** — 0–10 scale for anxiety, discomfort, or unease
- **Worry Hill** — habituation curve visual
- **Candy Jar** — metaphor for building non-threatening memories through exposure
- **Nickname the Fear** — child labels their anxiety with a personal nickname

### Cognitive Tools
- **Downward Arrow** — structured drill-down to surface the root feared outcome. Facilitated by parent or practitioner; practitioner approves. Has therapeutic value on its own.
- **Mottoes** — short cognitive correction phrases used between sessions

### Assessment and Monitoring Tools
- **Monitoring Form** — sent to parent before consultation 1. Parent monitors trigger situations, behaviors, and distress levels for one week. Delivered and collected through Float.
- **Pre-consultation Report** — generated from monitoring form data for practitioner reference
- **Session Notes** — free text, practitioner-only, captured during consultations and referenced in subsequent sessions

### Exposure Planning Tools
- **Trigger Situation List** — situations with DT ratings
- **Avoidance and Safety Behaviors** — per trigger situation, with DT ratings for refraining
- **Exposure Ladder** — rungs lowest to highest DT
- **Before Exposure** — feared outcome (from Downward Arrow), BIP, expected DT, confidence level (low/med/high), scheduled time, commit action
- **After Exposure** — did the feared outcome occur (observable), actual DT, BIP after, what learned
- **Adjust Flow** — patient flags "too hard"; practitioner receives alert and can adjust the exposure or message the patient
- **Action Plans** — generated summaries after consultation 3, one for child/teen and one for parent

### Parent Tools
- **Accommodation Behaviors** — identification and gradual reduction plan arranged as a ladder
- **Parent Module** — monitoring → reduction plan → non-accommodating responses → accept child's anxiety → express confidence child can cope

## 6. Platform Structure

### Practitioner Experience
- Referral intake and monitoring form dispatch
- Pre-consultation report from monitoring data
- Session notes capture and review
- Caseload view with patient status, completion alerts, missed experiment alerts
- Treatment plan configuration — trigger situations, behaviors, ladders, Downward Arrow
- Exposure planning and approval
- Action plan generation
- Progress monitoring — BIP and DT trends

### Parent Experience
- Monitoring form completion (pre-consultation)
- Psychoeducation on accommodation
- Accommodation module — identify, plan reduction, practice responses
- Visibility into child's exposure plan (configurable)
- Completion notifications

### Child / Teen / Young Adult Experience
- Psychoeducation — DT, Worry Hill, Candy Jar, Nickname
- My Plan — current ladder, where I am, what is next
- My Experiment — plan, commit, run, record
- Adjust flow — flag "too hard"
- Messages from practitioner
