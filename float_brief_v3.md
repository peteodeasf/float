# Float

## Product Brief

**Internal Working Document  —  Dr. Bridget Walker & Pete O'Dea  —  April 2026**

---

## 1. The Problem

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health problem in children and adolescents — affecting as many as one in three teens. CBT is the gold standard treatment, and its central component is structured, graduated exposure work. But the specific exposure-based model that actually produces results is practiced by only a small number of specialists nationwide.

Most practitioners — school social workers, school psychologists, community therapists — either lack proper training in exposure-based CBT or are using approaches that feel therapeutic but have significantly weaker outcomes. Many practitioners who believe they are delivering CBT are not using the exposure-based model that works. Others know the gap exists and refer to specialists, but availability is severely limited.

The result is a large and growing population of anxious children and teens who are not getting the treatment that would help them. This is not primarily a supply problem — it is a knowledge and method problem.

Parents are a critical and underutilized part of the treatment picture. Research shows that parental accommodation — the instinctive behaviors parents use to reduce a child's distress — actively maintains and worsens anxiety over time. Most parents don't know this, and most treatment models don't address it systematically.

---

## 2. The Solution

Float is a practitioner-led clinical platform that puts Dr. Walker's proven CBT model in the hands of every school social worker and community therapist treating anxious children and teens.

The platform does not replace the practitioner. It equips them — giving them the structure, tools, and between-session infrastructure to deliver outcomes that currently require specialist expertise. Dr. Walker's clinical model is encoded into the platform's logic, guiding practitioners through assessment, treatment planning, and the exposure experiment process.

Float connects three participants in the treatment: the practitioner, the parent, and the child or teen. The practitioner configures and monitors treatment. The parent is an active therapeutic agent with their own tools and behavioral targets. The child does the between-session therapeutic work using the platform. Float is the connective tissue between all three.

## 3. The Clinical Foundation

Float is built on Dr. Bridget Walker's CBT program for anxiety and related disorders in children, adolescents, and young adults, developed and refined through over 25 years of clinical practice. The model is documented in three published books:

- **Anxiety Relief for Kids, 2nd Edition** (New Harbinger, 2026) — the parent-facing guide to the full model, covering anxiety disorders broadly
- **Social Anxiety Relief for Teens** (New Harbinger, 2021) — the teen-facing guide to the social anxiety application of the model
- **Anxiety Relief for Kids: On-the-Spot Strategies to Help Your Child Overcome Worry, Panic & Avoidance** (New Harbinger, 2017) — parent-facing guide to the full model

The model centers on a structured, five-step process applied across anxiety disorder presentations:

1. Create a trigger situations list
2. Identify avoidance and safety behaviors
3. Build an exposure ladder
4. Run an exposure experiment
5. Climb more rungs on the ladder

For OCD specifically, the model uses Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) — structurally parallel to the exposure experiment model but targeting rituals rather than avoidance and safety behaviors. ERP is a distinct clinical track within the platform.

Two primary outcome measures are tracked throughout: the Distress Thermometer (0–10) and BIP — Belief in Prediction (0–100%). Reductions in both indicate progress and constitute the platform's core outcome data.

The Downward Arrow technique surfaces the underlying fear structure for each trigger situation, ensuring exposures target what the child actually fears rather than the surface presentation. It is used once per trigger situation.

The specific tools included in Float, and how they are applied in the platform, will be defined by Dr. Walker. The books represent self-help and parent-facing adaptations of the model; the platform will encode her clinical practice, which may differ in important ways.

## 4. The Clinical Toolkit

The following tools are present in Dr. Walker's published model. Which of these are encoded in Float, and in what form, is to be determined by Dr. Walker.

### Assessment and Monitoring Tools

- **Parent Monitoring Worksheet** — parent observes and records child's trigger situations, behaviors, and distress levels over time
- **Child Monitoring Worksheet** — age-appropriate self-monitoring for older children and teens
- **Distress Thermometer** — 0–10 scale for measuring anxiety/distress; used throughout the entire program
- **BIP (Belief in Prediction) rating** — 0–100% measure of belief in feared outcome; primary cognitive outcome measure

### Psychoeducation Tools

- **Worry Hill** — visual tool showing the habituation curve; explains to children why and how staying in a situation while refraining from engaging in safety or avoidance behaviors reduces anxiety
- **Candy Jar** — metaphor for building new non-threatening (safe) memories through exposures
- **Nickname the Fear** — child labels their anxiety with a personal nickname to maintain objectivity and reduce accommodation-seeking
- **Distress Thermometer** — child gives a subjective rating on a scale of 1–10 of their level of distress/anxiety in a given situation

### Cognitive Tools

- **Downward Arrow** — structured drill-down technique to surface the root fear beneath a surface worry; used once per trigger situation
- **Thought Bubbles** — visual tool to help children identify and gain objectivity about their thinking errors
- **Guided Discovery** — structured questioning technique for parents and practitioners to help children consider alternative outcomes and evaluate evidence for their fear-based beliefs
- **Mottoes** — short cognitive correction phrases used between sessions to manage thinking errors; explicitly not used during exposures

### Exposure Planning and Execution Tools

- **Trigger Situation List** — comprehensive list of various situations that set off anxiety and distress
- **Distress Thermometer** — used frequently in the program: to assign ratings for each situation, plan for exposures (distress level expected while refraining from avoidance and safety behaviors), assess outcomes of each exposure, and track progress
- **Avoidance and Safety Behaviors worksheet** — systematic inventory of behaviors by trigger situation, with distress thermometer ratings
- **Accommodation Behaviors worksheet** — parallel inventory for parents; tracks parental behaviors that maintain the child's anxiety
- **Exposures** — central to the process of conquering anxiety; each exposure consists of being in a trigger situation while refraining from engaging in preferred avoidance or safety behavior(s); each exposure is assigned the distress level expected
- **Exposure Ladder** — ranked hierarchy of exposures from lowest to highest distress thermometer rating
- **BIP (Belief in Prediction)** — assessed before each exposure, after each exposure, and as an outcome measure documenting changes in beliefs, which is an ultimate goal of exposure therapy
- **Before Exposure Worksheet** — structured planning for each experiment: what I plan to do, prediction, BIP rating, expected distress thermometer, tempting behaviors, confidence level
- **After Exposure Worksheet** — structured review: did the feared thing happen, actual distress thermometer, what I learned, updated BIP rating
- **Role-play Practice** — parent and child practice the exposure scenario before running it in real life
- **Weekly Check-In Worksheet** — structured weekly review of progress across exposures, behaviors, and accommodation

### OCD-Specific Tools

- **Rituals worksheet** — parallel to the avoidance/safety behaviors worksheet but tracking compulsions/rituals
- **Before/After ERP Worksheets** — identical structure to exposure worksheets but framed for ritual prevention
- **Feather Duster technique** — specific ERP approach for contamination obsessions

### Motivation Tools

- **Rewards system** — structured incentive planning; calibrated to difficulty of exposure; especially important for younger children
- **Hassles list** — how does your anxiety get in the way of doing what you want to do (go to birthday parties, participate in class, do desired activities)

## 5. How Float Works

Float operates on a two-phase rhythm for each patient.

### Setup Phase

Setup establishes the child's treatment plan. It may happen in session (in-person or remote) or asynchronously depending on the practitioner's workflow. The platform supports all modes.

During setup, monitoring and assessment are completed — identifying trigger situations, avoidance and safety behaviors (or rituals, for OCD), distress thermometer ratings, and the underlying fear structure via the Downward Arrow. The practitioner reviews this input, builds the exposure ladder, and configures the treatment plan. An AI review layer flags ladders that may need adjustment based on parameters defined by Dr. Walker, reducing the practitioner's cognitive load without replacing clinical judgment.

### Active Treatment Phase

Once configured, the child works through the treatment plan in sessions with the practitioner and also between sessions — planning and running exposure experiments or ERPs, recording distress thermometer and BIP ratings, and logging what they learned. The parent works on reducing accommodation behaviors using their own tools. The practitioner monitors asynchronously, reviews results before the next session, and adjusts the plan.

This cycle repeats across sessions — review, adjust, configure next steps — until the exposure ladder is complete and the child moves to maintenance.

## 6. Platform Structure

Float has three distinct user experiences — practitioner, parent, and child/teen/young adult — connected through shared clinical data and tools.

### Practitioner Experience

- **Caseload View**
- **Patient View**
- **Configuration View**

### Parent Experience

- **My Role**
- **Supporting My Child**

### Child / Teen / Young Adult Experience

- **My Plan**
- **My Experiment**

### Educational Content

Three distinct content streams:

- **Practitioner education** — building competency in the CBT model; training practitioners to use the tools correctly and understand the clinical reasoning behind each step
- **Parent psychoeducation** — explaining why accommodation maintains anxiety, how to respond differently, and how to actively support exposure work at home
- **Child/teen/young adult psychoeducation** — explaining why avoidance and safety behaviors make anxiety worse, why exposure experiments work, and building motivation to engage

Format, timing, and delivery of educational content to be defined by Dr. Walker.

## 7. Scope and Sequencing

Dr. Walker's model applies across anxiety disorder presentations in children, adolescents, and young adults, including social anxiety, perfectionism, separation anxiety, specific phobias, health anxiety, panic disorder, and OCD. Float is designed to support this full scope. Young adults use the same patient experience as teens — no separate UX is required.

Social anxiety is the recommended initial focus for Float's pilot and go-to-market. It is the most prevalent, best understood in the model, and most tractable for a school-based go-to-market. The platform architecture should be designed from the start to accommodate the fuller model — particularly the OCD/ERP track and the parent component — so that expanding scope does not require rebuilding.

The following are the key clinical and product decisions that require Dr. Walker's input before the platform can be fully designed.

### Clinical model and tools

- Which specific tools from the published model are in the platform versus handled verbally in session?
- Which tools are practitioner-facing only, which are child/teen-facing, and which are parent-facing?
- How is the Downward Arrow used in the platform — is it a tool the child or teen completes independently, or is it always facilitated by the practitioner or parent?
- What are the specific parameters for the AI review of exposure ladders — what defines a ladder that needs practitioner attention?
- Which exposure types belong in the platform at this stage — situational exposures only, or imaginal and interoceptive as well?
- How does she want to handle the Distress Thermometer vs. SUDS terminology — the books use both; which does she prefer for the platform?
- How is the Hassles list used in practice — at what point in the process, and who completes it?

### OCD and ERP track

- Is the OCD/ERP track in scope for the initial version of Float, or is it a later phase?
- Are there clinical considerations that make OCD a different enough population to warrant a separate go-to-market approach?

### Parent component

- How central is the parent component to the initial version — is it required for the model to work, or is it additive?
- For teen and young adult patients specifically, how does she think about the parent role — is it different from the child model?
- What does the parent-practitioner communication look like in her current practice?

### Practitioner workflow

- How much of setup does she expect children/teens/young adults to complete independently versus with practitioner or parent guidance?
- Does the practitioner always review and approve the exposure ladder before the patient starts running experiments?
- What does the between-session practitioner role look like in practice — how actively does she expect practitioners to monitor and respond?

### Educational content

- What psychoeducation does a child or teen need before they begin setup?
- What do practitioners need to learn, and what is the right format?
- What does she consider the most important things parents need to understand about their role?
