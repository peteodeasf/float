# Float

## Product Brief

**Internal Working Document  —  Dr. Bridget Walker & Pete O'Dea  —  April 2026**

---

## 1. The Problem

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health problem in children and adolescents — affecting as many as one in three teens. CBT is the gold standard treatment, and its central component is structured, graduated exposure work. But the specific exposure-based model that actually produces results is practiced by only a small number of specialists nationwide.

Most practitioners — school social workers, school psychologists, community therapists — either lack proper training in exposure-based CBT or are using approaches that feel therapeutic but have significantly weaker outcomes. Many practitioners who believe they are delivering CBT are not using the exposure-based model that works. Others know the gap exists and refer to specialists, but availability is severely limited.

The result is a large and growing population of anxious children and teens who are not getting the treatment that would help them. This is not primarily a supply problem — it is a knowledge and method problem.

Parents are a critical and underutilized part of the treatment picture. Research shows that family accommodation — parental and sibling behaviors that reduce a child's distress — actively maintains and worsens anxiety over time. This is nearly universal: 95–100% of parents of anxious youth engage in accommodation behaviors regularly, often daily. Most parents don't know this, and most treatment models don't address it systematically.

---

## 2. The Solution

Float is a practitioner-led clinical platform that puts Dr. Walker's proven CBT model in the hands of every school social worker and community therapist treating anxious children and teens.

The platform does not replace the practitioner. It equips them — giving them the structure, tools, and between-session infrastructure to deliver outcomes that currently require specialist expertise. Dr. Walker's clinical model is encoded into the platform's logic, guiding practitioners through assessment, treatment planning, and the exposure experiment process.

Float connects three participants in the treatment: the practitioner, the parent, and the child or teen. The practitioner configures and monitors treatment. The parent is an active therapeutic agent with their own tools and behavioral targets. The child does the between-session therapeutic work using the platform. Float is the connective tissue between all three.

## 3. The Clinical Foundation

Float is built on Dr. Bridget Walker's CBT program for anxiety and related disorders in children, adolescents, and young adults, developed and refined through over 25 years of clinical practice. The model is documented in three published books:

- **Anxiety Relief for Kids, 2nd Edition** (New Harbinger, 2026) — the parent-facing guide to the full model, covering anxiety disorders broadly
- **Social Anxiety Relief for Teens** (New Harbinger, 2021) — the teen-facing guide to the social anxiety application of the model
- **Anxiety Relief for Kids: On-the-Spot Strategies to Help Your Child Overcome Worry, Panic & Avoidance** (New Harbinger, 2017) — parent-facing guide to the full model

The model centers on a structured process applied across anxiety disorder presentations. The core sequence for each trigger situation is:

1. Build a trigger situations list with Distress Thermometer (DT) ratings for each situation
2. Select the situation with the lowest DT rating that occurs frequently
3. Identify the avoidance and safety behaviors the child relies on in that situation
4. Assess the child's DT for being in the trigger situation while refraining from each behavior — each of these ratings becomes a rung on the exposure ladder
5. Use the Downward Arrow technique on each rung to identify the child's feared outcome — what they believe will happen if they can't avoid the situation or use their safety behaviors
6. Arrange the rungs from lowest to highest DT — the resulting Exposure Ladder guides the exposure sequence
7. Begin exposures at the bottom rung (lowest DT), climbing each rung until mastery, then moving to the next
8. Once a ladder is complete, move to the next trigger situation and repeat

**Belief in Prediction (BIP)** is derived from the Downward Arrow: after identifying the child's most feared outcome, ask how strongly they believe that outcome will occur (0–100%). A BIP of 60% means the child believes that in 10 situations, 6 times the feared outcome will happen. BIP is assessed before and after each exposure and is a primary outcome measure.

**Two primary outcome measures** are tracked throughout: the Distress Thermometer (0–10) and BIP (0–100%). The terminology is Distress Thermometer — not SUDS — because some anxious children present with discomfort, dislike, or unease rather than overt fear.

For OCD specifically, the model uses Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP). OCD is out of scope for the initial version given the social anxiety focus.

## 4. The Clinical Toolkit

The following tools are confirmed for the platform. All tools are accessible to practitioners, parents, and children/teens — none are practitioner-only. Tools are introduced and explained by practitioners and/or parents in various settings: individual sessions, home, school counselor settings, and group settings. Practice exercises are available for each user type.

### Psychoeducation Tools

- **Distress Thermometer (DT)** — 0–10 scale measuring anxiety, discomfort, dislike, or unease. Used throughout the entire program. Preferred over SUDS because it captures the full range of anxious presentations, not just fear.
- **Worry Hill** — visual tool showing the habituation curve; explains to children why staying in a situation while refraining from avoidance and safety behaviors reduces anxiety over time
- **Candy Jar** — metaphor for building new non-threatening memories through exposures
- **Nickname the Fear** — child labels their anxiety with a personal nickname to maintain objectivity and reduce accommodation-seeking

### Cognitive Tools

- **Downward Arrow** — structured drill-down technique used per rung to surface the root feared outcome. Can be facilitated by a parent or practitioner; practitioner must approve that the feared outcome is drilled down as far as possible (e.g., "I could die," "I will be a failure and be lonely," "I will be rejected and no one will like me"). Has therapeutic value on its own — a mild form of exposure because the child thinks and talks about the feared outcome rather than avoiding it. Psychoeducation module required for all users; practitioners need practice exercises and quizzes.
- **Thought Bubbles** — visual tool to help children identify and gain objectivity about their thinking errors
- **Guided Discovery** — structured questioning technique for parents and practitioners to help children consider alternative outcomes
- **Mottoes** — short cognitive correction phrases used between sessions to manage thinking errors; not used during exposures

### Assessment and Monitoring Tools

- **Trigger Situation List** — comprehensive list of situations that set off anxiety and distress, each rated with the DT
- **Parent Monitoring Worksheet** — parent observes and records child's trigger situations, behaviors, and distress levels
- **Child Monitoring Worksheet** — age-appropriate self-monitoring for older children and teens

### Exposure Planning and Execution Tools

- **Avoidance and Safety Behaviors Worksheet** — systematic inventory of behaviors by trigger situation, with DT ratings for refraining from each
- **Exposure Ladder** — rungs arranged from lowest to highest DT; exposures begin at the bottom rung and climb to the top
- **BIP (Belief in Prediction)** — 0–100% rating of belief in the most feared outcome, derived from the Downward Arrow. Assessed before each exposure, after each exposure, and tracked as a primary outcome measure
- **Before Exposure Worksheet** — what I plan to do, my prediction (the feared outcome from the Downward Arrow), BIP rating, expected DT, behaviors I might be tempted to do, confidence level
- **After Exposure Worksheet** — did the feared outcome occur (must be observable by all, not based on feelings), actual DT, what I learned, updated BIP rating
- **Role-play Practice** — parent and child practice the exposure scenario before running it in real life
- **Weekly Check-In Worksheet** — structured weekly review of progress

### Parent Tools

- **Accommodation Behaviors Worksheet** — identifies parental (and sibling) accommodation behaviors with DT ratings for gradually relinquishing each; arranged as an accommodation ladder from lowest to highest DT
- **Parent Module sequence**: (1) identify accommodation behaviors using monitoring, (2) develop a gradual reduction plan with child's DT ratings, (3) generate and practice non-accommodating responses, (4) learn to accept child's anxiety without frustration, (5) develop ability to express confidence that the child can cope without parent intervention

### Motivation Tools

- **Rewards system** — structured incentive planning calibrated to difficulty of exposure; especially important for younger children
- **Hassles list** — how does your anxiety get in the way of doing what you want to do

### OCD-Specific Tools
*(Out of scope for initial version — social anxiety focus)*

## 5. How Float Works

Float operates on a two-phase rhythm for each patient.

### Setup Phase

Setup establishes the child's treatment plan. It may happen in session (in-person or remote) or asynchronously depending on the practitioner's workflow and the child's age and maturity. For very young children, parents and practitioners provide significant support. For older, self-motivated teens, more independent completion is possible.

Factors affecting level of support needed: age of child, maturity, motivation, and co-morbid ADHD (which makes it harder to keep track and remember to do exposures).

During setup:
- Trigger situations are identified and rated with the DT
- Avoidance and safety behaviors are identified for each situation
- DT ratings are collected for refraining from each behavior
- The Downward Arrow is used per rung to identify the feared outcome
- The exposure ladder is assembled — rungs arranged lowest to highest DT
- BIP is established from the Downward Arrow result
- The practitioner reviews and approves the ladder before the child begins exposures (recommended best practice)
- An AI review layer flags ladders that may need adjustment

### Active Treatment Phase

Once configured, the child works through the treatment plan in sessions with the practitioner and between sessions. Exposures must be planned ahead for specific times and days — with the practitioner and/or parent, or independently for self-motivated and mature patients.

**Between-session accountability is central to success.** Key workflows:

- When a child completes an exposure (with DT and BIP entries), the practitioner receives a notification
- If the child agrees, the parent also receives a completion notification
- If a planned exposure time passes without completion, the practitioner is notified to follow up — the practitioner can then message the child to ask what happened and make adjustments
- Practitioners can supervise first exposures remotely via FaceTime, Zoom, or phone — first attempts are best done in the presence of a practitioner
- Once a child does an exposure, they typically learn it was not as challenging as predicted and gain confidence to continue independently
- Practitioner check-in frequency is configurable — ideally daily, minimum weekly
- For younger children, parents communicate DT and BIP to the practitioner on the child's behalf

The parent works simultaneously on reducing accommodation behaviors using the parent module. The practitioner monitors all activity asynchronously and adjusts the plan as needed.

This cycle repeats — review, adjust, configure next steps — until the exposure ladder is complete and the child moves to the next trigger situation. Once all ladders are complete, the child moves to maintenance.

## 6. Platform Structure

Float has three distinct user experiences — practitioner, parent, and child/teen/young adult — connected through shared clinical data and tools.

### Practitioner Experience

- **Caseload View** — all active patients with status indicators, last experiment date, completion notifications, and missed experiment alerts
- **Patient View** — pre-session brief, treatment plan status, experiment history, DT and BIP trends, parent accommodation progress
- **Configuration View** — trigger situation setup, Downward Arrow facilitation, ladder builder, ladder review and approval

### Parent Experience

- **My Role** — psychoeducation on accommodation and why it maintains anxiety
- **Accommodation Module** — identify accommodation behaviors, DT ratings for refraining, gradual reduction plan arranged as an accommodation ladder
- **Supporting My Child** — visibility into child's exposure plan (configurable), completion notifications, guidance on responding without accommodating

### Child / Teen / Young Adult Experience

- **My Plan** — current ladder, where I am, what is next
- **My Experiment** — plan the exposure (feared outcome from Downward Arrow, BIP, expected DT, confidence), commit, run it, record results (did the feared thing happen — must be observable, not based on feelings — actual DT, what I learned, updated BIP)

### Educational Content

Three distinct content streams with practice exercises for each:

- **Practitioner education** — building competency in the CBT model; Downward Arrow psychoeducation and practice required; practitioner exercises and quizzes
- **Parent psychoeducation** — explaining why accommodation maintains anxiety, how to respond differently, how to actively support exposure work, how to express confidence in child's ability to cope
- **Child/teen/young adult psychoeducation** — Distress Thermometer, Worry Hill, Candy Jar, Nickname the Fear, Mottoes; why avoidance makes anxiety worse; why exposure experiments work; building motivation to engage

Format, timing, and sequencing of educational content to be defined with Dr. Walker.

## 7. Scope and Sequencing

**Initial focus: Social anxiety.** Dr. Walker's model applies across anxiety disorder presentations in children, adolescents, and young adults. The initial pilot focuses on social anxiety — the most prevalent, best understood in the model, and most tractable for a school-based go-to-market.

**OCD/ERP:** Out of scope for the initial version. To be added in a later phase.

**Young adults:** Use the same patient experience as teens — no separate UX required.

**Platform architecture** should be designed from the start to accommodate the fuller model so that expanding scope does not require rebuilding.
